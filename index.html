Detailed Project on River Pollution: Comparison of the Ganga (India) and the Thames (UK)

Introduction

Rivers are essential to both the environment and human society, serving as sources of drinking water, irrigation, and industrial processes, as well as supporting diverse ecosystems. This project presents a comprehensive comparison of the Ganga River in India and the Thames River in the UK. It examines their current pollution levels and those from a decade ago, focusing on pesticides, heavy metals, organic waste, chemical waste, and direct sewage discharge. We also evaluate the water quality, potability, and impact on aquatic life. Additionally, we explore the actions taken by individuals, communities, and governments to improve these rivers' conditions.

The Ganga River (India)

Current Condition (2023)

Pesticides

The Ganga River faces severe pesticide pollution primarily due to agricultural runoff. Pesticides such as DDT, HCH, and other organochlorines are commonly used in the agricultural fields surrounding the river basin. Studies have shown that concentrations of DDT can reach up to 1.2 µg/L, while HCH levels are around 0.9 µg/L in certain stretches of the river. These persistent organic pollutants (POPs) are resistant to environmental degradation, leading to bioaccumulation in the food chain. This poses significant health risks to humans and aquatic life, including hormonal disruptions and increased cancer risk.

Heavy Metals

Heavy metal contamination is a critical issue for the Ganga River. Industrial activities, mining operations, and agricultural runoff contribute to high levels of arsenic, lead, mercury, and cadmium in the river water. Arsenic levels range from 0.05 to 0.1 mg/L, significantly higher than the World Health Organization’s (WHO) safe limit of 0.01 mg/L for drinking water. Lead concentrations vary between 0.02 and 0.05 mg/L. These heavy metals can cause severe health problems, including neurological damage, kidney failure, and developmental issues in children. The contamination also affects the river’s biodiversity, reducing fish populations and impacting aquatic plants.

Organic Waste

Organic waste pollution in the Ganga is predominantly due to the discharge of untreated or partially treated sewage and the disposal of waste from religious activities. The Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD) levels, which indicate the amount of organic matter in water, are alarmingly high, often exceeding 5 mg/L in many areas. High BOD levels deplete dissolved oxygen in the water, making it difficult for aquatic organisms to survive. This organic pollution is exacerbated during festivals when large quantities of biodegradable materials, flowers, and ashes are thrown into the river.

Chemical Waste

Industrial effluents are a major source of chemical waste in the Ganga. Tanneries, paper mills, textile factories, and other industries discharge untreated or inadequately treated wastewater containing hazardous chemicals, dyes, and solvents into the river. The presence of these chemicals leads to water toxicity, affecting both human health and the ecosystem. For instance, chromium from tanneries can cause severe skin conditions and respiratory problems, while other chemicals may disrupt endocrine systems in aquatic organisms.

Direct Sewage Discharge

Direct sewage discharge remains a significant problem for the Ganga River. Despite efforts to increase sewage treatment capacity, approximately 2.9 billion liters of untreated sewage are still discharged into the river daily. This untreated sewage contains not only organic waste but also pathogens, heavy metals, and nutrients that lead to eutrophication. The proliferation of harmful algal blooms from nutrient overload further depletes oxygen levels, creating dead zones where aquatic life cannot survive. This contamination severely affects the river’s potability and poses serious health risks to communities relying on the river for their daily water needs.

Water Quality and Aquatic Life

The cumulative effect of these pollutants is a substantial decline in water quality, rendering it unsafe for drinking and detrimental to aquatic life. The river’s ecosystem has been severely impacted, with many fish species facing extinction due to the toxic environment. The biodiversity of the Ganga has decreased, and the health of surviving aquatic species is compromised, affecting the entire food web.

Condition a Decade Ago (2013)

Pesticides

A decade ago, pesticide pollution in the Ganga was present but less severe compared to today. The levels of DDT and HCH were lower, reflecting somewhat less intensive agricultural practices. However, the awareness and regulation of pesticide use were not as robust, leading to a gradual increase in contamination over the years.

Heavy Metals

Heavy metal contamination was also significant a decade ago. Industrial regulations were not as stringent, leading to higher emissions of arsenic, lead, and mercury into the river. Monitoring and remediation efforts were limited, contributing to the accumulation of these metals in the river ecosystem.

Organic Waste

Organic waste pollution was consistently high, driven by inadequate waste management infrastructure. The BOD levels were similar to current figures, reflecting the persistent challenge of untreated sewage and waste from religious activities. Efforts to improve waste management were in their early stages, leading to limited progress.

Chemical Waste

Industrial pollution was prevalent, with many industries discharging untreated effluents into the river. Regulatory frameworks were less effective, and enforcement was weak, allowing for significant chemical waste to enter the Ganga. The impact on water quality and aquatic life was severe, contributing to the river's declining health.

Direct Sewage Discharge

Untreated sewage discharge was widespread, with limited sewage treatment facilities available. The infrastructure for sewage management was underdeveloped, leading to significant volumes of untreated sewage entering the river. This contributed to high levels of pathogens and organic pollutants in the water.

Water Quality and Aquatic Life

The water quality of the Ganga a decade ago was poor, with high levels of contaminants affecting its potability and safety. The aquatic life in the river was already in decline, with many species struggling to survive in the polluted environment. The biodiversity of the river was decreasing, reflecting the severe impact of pollution on the ecosystem.

Actions Taken

Government Initiatives

To address the severe pollution of the Ganga, the Indian government launched several initiatives. The most notable is the Namami Gange Programme, initiated in 2015. This comprehensive program aims to clean and rejuvenate the river through various measures, including improving sewage treatment infrastructure, regulating industrial effluents, and promoting sustainable agricultural practices. The program has led to the establishment and upgrading of numerous sewage treatment plants (STPs) in major cities along the Ganga, enhancing their capacity and operational efficiency.

The government has also implemented stricter regulations on industrial discharges. Industries are required to treat their effluents to meet specified standards before discharge. Regular monitoring and stringent penalties for non-compliance are enforced to ensure adherence to these regulations. Additionally, there have been efforts to promote eco-friendly practices in agriculture, such as integrated pest management and the use of organic fertilizers, to reduce pesticide runoff.

Community and Individual Actions

At the community level, various NGOs and local groups have been actively involved in awareness campaigns and river-cleaning activities. These initiatives aim to educate the public about the importance of the river and the impact of pollution. Community-driven clean-up drives are regularly organized, engaging local residents, students, and volunteers in efforts to remove waste and debris from the riverbanks.

Individuals have also played a role by participating in clean-up drives and reducing their plastic usage. Public awareness programs have encouraged people to adopt eco-friendly practices in their daily lives, such as minimizing the use of single-use plastics and properly disposing of waste. Religious leaders and organizations have been involved

Religious leaders and organizations have been involved in promoting environmentally friendly practices during festivals and religious ceremonies. They encourage devotees to use biodegradable materials and avoid immersing non-biodegradable items in the river. This collaborative effort has helped reduce the pollution load during major religious events.

The Thames River (UK)

Current Condition (2023)

Pesticides

The Thames River in the UK also faces challenges with pesticide pollution, although to a lesser extent compared to the Ganga. Agricultural runoff is the primary source, with pesticides like neonicotinoids and glyphosate being detected in the water. These chemicals can harm aquatic insects and other wildlife, but regulatory measures have helped to keep their concentrations below hazardous levels for human health.

Heavy Metals

Heavy metal contamination in the Thames arises from historical industrial activities and urban runoff. Metals such as cadmium, lead, and mercury are found in sediment and water samples. While current levels are generally within safe limits for human health, they can still pose risks to aquatic life, particularly benthic organisms that live in or near the riverbed.

Organic Waste

Organic waste pollution in the Thames has been significantly reduced through effective wastewater treatment and strict regulations. However, occasional combined sewer overflows (CSOs) during heavy rainfall can lead to temporary spikes in organic matter, affecting water quality and dissolved oxygen levels.

Chemical Waste

Chemical waste from industrial and urban sources remains a concern for the Thames. Efforts to control and treat industrial discharges have been successful, but emerging pollutants like pharmaceuticals and personal care products are increasingly detected in the water. These substances can disrupt endocrine systems in aquatic life and accumulate in the food chain.

Direct Sewage Discharge

Direct sewage discharge into the Thames is rare due to the comprehensive sewage treatment infrastructure. However, incidents of untreated sewage entering the river still occur during extreme weather events. These incidents are closely monitored and addressed to minimize their impact on water quality and public health.

Water Quality and Aquatic Life

The water quality of the Thames has improved significantly over the past few decades. It is now considered one of the cleanest metropolitan rivers in the world. The recovery of the river has led to a resurgence in biodiversity, with species such as salmon, otters, and various bird species returning to the river. Continuous monitoring and management efforts ensure that the Thames remains a healthy and vibrant ecosystem.

Condition a Decade Ago (2013)

Pesticides

A decade ago, the Thames experienced higher levels of pesticide pollution due to less stringent agricultural practices. Efforts to regulate and reduce pesticide usage have since improved water quality, benefiting both human health and the river's ecosystem.

Heavy Metals

Heavy metal contamination was more pronounced ten years ago. Since then, remediation efforts and stricter regulations have reduced the levels of these contaminants, although legacy pollution from historical industrial activities remains a challenge.

Organic Waste

Organic waste pollution has been a long-standing issue for the Thames. A decade ago, CSOs were more frequent, leading to higher BOD levels and lower dissolved oxygen concentrations. Investments in upgrading wastewater infrastructure have since mitigated these problems, resulting in improved water quality.

Chemical Waste

Industrial and urban chemical waste contributed significantly to pollution in the Thames a decade ago. Ongoing efforts to regulate discharges and treat wastewater have reduced the presence of harmful chemicals, although emerging pollutants continue to pose new challenges.

Direct Sewage Discharge

Incidents of direct sewage discharge were more common ten years ago due to outdated infrastructure and extreme weather events. Modernization of the sewage system has greatly reduced these occurrences, protecting both the river and public health.

Water Quality and Aquatic Life

The water quality of the Thames was poorer a decade ago, with higher levels of contaminants affecting aquatic life. Restoration efforts have since led to a dramatic improvement in biodiversity and overall river health, showcasing the success of targeted environmental management.

Actions Taken

Government Initiatives

The UK government has implemented several measures to protect and restore the Thames. The establishment of the Thames Tideway Tunnel project, a significant infrastructure investment, aims to reduce CSOs and improve water quality. This “super sewer” will intercept and store excess sewage and rainwater during heavy rainfall, preventing untreated sewage from entering the river.

Environmental regulations, such as the Water Framework Directive, have also been instrumental in improving the river’s health. These regulations set targets for water quality and biodiversity, with regular monitoring and reporting to ensure compliance. The government works closely with environmental agencies, local authorities, and stakeholders to achieve these goals.

Community and Individual Actions

Community involvement has been a cornerstone of efforts to protect the Thames. Organizations such as the Thames21 charity engage volunteers in river clean-up activities, habitat restoration, and educational programs. These initiatives raise awareness and foster a sense of stewardship among local communities.

Individuals contribute by participating in clean-up events, reducing plastic use, and supporting policies aimed at environmental protection. Public campaigns highlight the importance of keeping the river clean and the role that every individual can play in preserving this vital natural resource.
